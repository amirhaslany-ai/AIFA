# Viral Psychology
**Document ID:** `03_IdeaEngine/Viral_Psychology.md`
**Referenced by:** `03_IdeaEngine/README.md` (Knowledge Base), `Hook_System.md`

> General, well-established audience-engagement principles this engine draws on when choosing angles and hook directions. These are the *why* behind `Hook_System.md`'s hook types and `Trend_Scoring.md`'s engagement factor — not persona-specific content, so nothing here needs founder input.

## Principles used in angle and hook selection

- **Curiosity gap** — a piece of information is withheld or implied but not given, creating a specific, answerable question the viewer wants resolved. Effective because it's a completable itch, not vague intrigue; the gap must be closeable within the content, not a bait-and-switch (which would violate the honesty mandate in `01_Core/Vision.md`).
- **Pattern interrupt** — something that breaks an expected sequence (a claim that contradicts what the viewer assumes, an unexpected visual or framing) grabs attention because it doesn't match a predicted pattern. Most useful in the first 5–8 seconds, per the hook window in `01_Core/Quality_Standards.md`.
- **Stakes / relevance** — "why this matters to *you*, specifically" outperforms "why this is generically interesting." Course and Tool Review content lean on this heavily (a viewer needs to know what breaks or improves for them).
- **Specificity over vagueness** — a specific claim ("this one setting cuts your API cost by 40%") earns more trust and attention than a vague one ("there are ways to save money"). Specificity is also what keeps an angle honest and falsifiable, tying directly to the factual-anchor requirement in `README.md`.
- **Social proof / consensus signal** — framing that shows other credible people/sources are paying attention to this too (without needing to name-drop) can raise perceived importance. Must stay honest — this is about signaling genuine relevance, not manufacturing false consensus.
- **Contrarian / expectation-violation** — a claim that pushes back against a popular assumption is inherently more engaging than one that confirms it, *if and only if* the underlying facts actually support the contrarian read. Using this lever without factual support is exactly the "hype-only angle" the Decision Rules in `README.md` forbid.
- **Simplicity of the core claim** — one clear idea a viewer can restate to someone else afterward beats several ideas that blur together. This is why `README.md`'s Decision Rules require one angle per brief.

## How this maps to the pipeline

- **Angle selection** (`Topic_Selection.md`) draws on curiosity gap, specificity, and stakes/relevance to judge which candidate angle is strongest.
- **Hook direction selection** (`Hook_System.md`) maps each of these principles to a concrete hook type.
- **Trend scoring** (`Trend_Scoring.md`) includes an engagement-potential factor informed by how strongly a candidate can use these levers honestly.

## Guardrail

Every principle above is subordinate to the honesty mandate (`01_Core/Vision.md`, `AIFA_CONSTITUTION.md` Core Principle 1). A technique that only works by overstating, omitting material context, or implying something the facts don't support is not a valid use of that technique here — it's rejected at the Decision Rules stage in `README.md`, regardless of how well it would score for engagement.
